### Title
Truncating Integer Division in DAO Accumulate Rate and Withdrawal Calculation Causes Permanent Shannon Leakage into Unclaimable `S` Accumulator - (File: `util/dao/src/lib.rs`)

---

### Summary

In `dao_field_with_current_epoch()` and `calculate_maximum_withdraw()`, truncating integer division is used at two compounding points: the `ar` (accumulate rate) increase and the withdrawal capacity calculation. Because `ar` grows slightly slower than the mathematically exact value while `S` grows slightly faster (via the complementary rounding), a systematic gap opens between what `S` holds and what depositors can ever claim. The "leftover" shannons accumulate in `S` permanently and are unreachable by any party.

---

### Finding Description

**Point 1 — `ar_increase` truncation in `dao_field_with_current_epoch()`** [1](#0-0) 

```rust
let ar_increase128 =
    u128::from(parent_ar) * u128::from(current_g2.as_u64()) / u128::from(parent_c.as_u64());
```

The true increase is `parent_ar × g2 / C` (exact). Truncation makes `ar` grow by at most 1 less per block than it should.

**Point 2 — `miner_issuance` truncation (complementary rounding)** [2](#0-1) 

```rust
let miner_issuance128 = u128::from(current_g2.as_u64()) * u128::from(parent_u.as_u64())
    / u128::from(parent_c.as_u64());
...
let nervosdao_issuance = current_g2.safe_sub(miner_issuance)?;
```

Because `miner_issuance = floor(g2 × U / C)`, the NervosDAO receives `g2 − floor(g2 × U / C) ≥ g2 × (C−U)/C`. So `S` grows slightly *faster* than the exact share, while `ar` grows slightly *slower*. The gap between what `S` holds and what `ar` entitles depositors to claim widens every block.

**Point 3 — `withdraw_counted_capacity` truncation in `calculate_maximum_withdraw()`** [3](#0-2) 

```rust
let withdraw_counted_capacity = u128::from(counted_capacity.as_u64())
    * u128::from(withdrawing_ar)
    / u128::from(deposit_ar);
let withdraw_capacity =
    Capacity::shannons(withdraw_counted_capacity as u64).safe_add(occupied_capacity)?;
```

The depositor receives `floor(counted_capacity × withdrawing_ar / deposit_ar)`. The truncated remainder stays in `S` and is never returned to the depositor.

The `safe_mul_ratio` helper used for proposer reward splits avoids this problem by computing `tx_fee − proposer_share` (so the miner absorbs the remainder), but the DAO path has no such complementary assignment for the depositor. [4](#0-3) 

---

### Impact Explanation

Every DAO withdrawal permanently loses up to 1 shannon to the `S` accumulator. Because `ar` is also systematically underestimated (Point 1), the total interest claimable across all depositors is always less than what `S` holds. The difference is locked in `S` forever — no depositor, miner, or governance mechanism can recover it. With millions of DAO deposits over the life of the chain, the aggregate locked amount is non-trivial.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

This affects every single DAO withdrawal. Any unprivileged user who deposits CKB into the NervosDAO and later withdraws triggers this path. The entry point is a standard phase-2 DAO withdrawal transaction submitted by any transaction sender. No special privilege is required.

---

### Recommendation

For `calculate_maximum_withdraw()`, consider returning the exact remaining `S` balance to the last withdrawer in an epoch (analogous to the `remainder_reward` mechanism used for primary block rewards in `EpochExt::block_reward()`). [5](#0-4) 

For `ar_increase`, accumulate the per-block truncation remainder in a separate field and apply it to the next block's `ar` update, preventing systematic drift.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Deposit `counted_capacity = 1_000_000_000` shannons (10 CKB) into the DAO at block `deposit_header` where `deposit_ar = 10_000_000_000_000_001`.
2. Withdraw at block `withdrawing_header` where `withdrawing_ar = 10_000_000_001_000_001`.
3. Compute:
   - `withdraw_counted_capacity = floor(1_000_000_000 × 10_000_000_001_000_001 / 10_000_000_000_000_001)`
   - Exact value: `1_000_000_000.1000000000000...`
   - Truncated: `1_000_000_000`
   - Lost: 1 shannon, permanently locked in `S`.
4. Multiply across millions of withdrawals: the `S` accumulator holds shannons that no withdrawal formula can ever reach, because `ar` never catches up to the exact ratio. [6](#0-5) [7](#0-6)
