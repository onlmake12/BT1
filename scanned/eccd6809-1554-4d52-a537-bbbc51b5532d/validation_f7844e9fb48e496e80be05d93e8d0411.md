### Title
Unbounded `pending_get_block_proposals` accumulation causes single oversized `fetch_txs` call in `prune_tx_proposal_request`, stalling the tx pool service — (`sync/src/relayer/mod.rs`)

### Summary

`prune_tx_proposal_request` drains the entire, unbounded `pending_get_block_proposals` map and issues a **single** `fetch_txs` call with all accumulated `ProposalShortId`s. Because the tx pool service is a single-threaded actor, this one call monopolizes the service for O(M) time, queuing all other operations (block template generation, tx admission, RPC queries) behind it.

### Finding Description

**Accumulation path — no size cap on `pending_get_block_proposals`**

`pending_get_block_proposals` is a bare `DashMap` with no upper-bound on entries. [1](#0-0) 

`insert_get_block_proposals` inserts every not-found proposal ID from every peer with no eviction or cap: [2](#0-1) 

**Per-message flow feeds the map**

In `GetBlockProposalProcess::execute`, proposals not found in the tx pool are cached into `pending_get_block_proposals`: [3](#0-2) 

The per-message limit is `max_block_proposals_limit × max_uncles_num` — a consensus constant, not a small number — and it applies per message, not to the total map size. [4](#0-3) 

**Timer drains everything into one unbounded call**

When `TX_PROPOSAL_TOKEN` fires, `prune_tx_proposal_request` clones and clears the entire map, then issues **one** `fetch_txs` call with all M accumulated IDs: [5](#0-4) 

`drain_get_block_proposals` has no truncation: [6](#0-5) 

**Tx pool service is a single-threaded actor**

`FetchTxs` is processed sequentially in the tx pool service loop: [7](#0-6) 

The service holds `tx_pool.read()` (or equivalent) while iterating over all M short IDs. Every other message — `BlockTemplate`, `SubmitRemoteTx`, `GetTxStatus`, etc. — queues behind it: [8](#0-7) 

### Impact Explanation

While the `fetch_txs` call is in flight, the tx pool service cannot process any other message. This causes:
- Block template generation stalls (miner cannot assemble blocks)
- Remote tx admission stalls (mempool growth halts)
- RPC queries (`get_transaction`, `get_tx_pool_info`) time out

This directly maps to the stated scope: **network congestion and block assembly delays**.

### Likelihood Explanation

**Rate limiter exists but does not bound total accumulation.** The rate limiter is 30 req/s per `(PeerIndex, message_type)`: [9](#0-8) 

With `MAX_RELAY_PEERS = 128` peers: [10](#0-9) 

Each peer can send 3 `GetBlockProposal` messages per 100 ms timer interval, each carrying up to `max_block_proposals_limit × max_uncles_num` IDs. With random/fake IDs (none present in the tx pool), all IDs are cached. The attacker needs to sustain enough peer connections and bandwidth to fill the map to a size where a single `fetch_txs` call causes a measurable stall. This is achievable with a modest botnet or even Sybil peers on a well-connected node.

### Recommendation

1. **Cap `pending_get_block_proposals`**: enforce a hard maximum (e.g., `max_block_proposals_limit`) on the total number of entries in the map, dropping or ignoring excess insertions.
2. **Chunk the `fetch_txs` call**: in `prune_tx_proposal_request`, split the drained IDs into fixed-size batches and issue multiple `fetch_txs` calls, yielding between batches.
3. **Per-peer accounting**: track how many pending IDs each peer has contributed and evict the largest contributors when the cap is reached.

### Proof of Concept

1. Connect 128 Sybil peers to the victim node.
2. Each peer sends `GetBlockProposal` messages at 30 req/s with random, disjoint `ProposalShortId`s (10 bytes each, not present in the tx pool).
3. Over one 100 ms timer interval, up to ~1 M unique IDs accumulate in `pending_get_block_proposals`.
4. `TX_PROPOSAL_TOKEN` fires → `drain_get_block_proposals` returns ~1 M entries → `fetch_txs` is called with a ~1 M-element `HashSet`.
5. Measure: issue a `get_block_template` RPC call concurrently; observe it stalls until the `fetch_txs` call completes.
6. Assert: stall duration scales linearly with M and exceeds any safe threshold.
