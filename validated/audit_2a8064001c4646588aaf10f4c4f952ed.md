### Title
Stakers Can Escape Slashing by Undelegating Before Per-Staker `slash` Is Applied — (`governance/pyth_staking_sdk/src/idl/integrity-pool.json`)

---

### Summary

The Oracle Integrity Staking (OIS) integrity pool applies slashing **per-staker** via a two-step process: `create_slash_event` (creates the event on-chain) followed by individual `slash` calls (one per `stakeAccountPositions`). Because the `undelegate` instruction does not require the `delegationRecord` account and therefore cannot enforce that all pending slash events have been applied, a delegator who observes a `create_slash_event` transaction on-chain can immediately call `undelegate` to exit their position before the `slash` instruction is executed against them. Stakers who react quickly escape the penalty entirely; stakers who do not react (or cannot) bear the full intended slash burden, but the DAO receives less slashed PYTH than intended.

---

### Finding Description

The integrity pool uses a two-phase slashing design:

**Phase 1 — `create_slash_event`:** The `reward_program_authority` calls this instruction, which creates a `SlashEvent` PDA keyed by `(publisher, index)` storing `epoch`, `slash_ratio`, and `slash_custody`. It also increments `num_slash_events[publisher_index]` in `pool_data`. [1](#0-0) 

**Phase 2 — `slash`:** This instruction must be called **once per delegator** to actually reduce that delegator's position. It requires `delegation_record` (writable) — a per-staker PDA that tracks `nextSlashEventIndex` — and `stakeAccountPositions` (writable). [2](#0-1) 

The `delegationRecord` struct tracks `lastEpoch` and `nextSlashEventIndex`, meaning slashes are applied lazily, one staker at a time. [3](#0-2) 

**The gap:** The `undelegate` instruction's account list does **not** include `delegation_record` or `slash_event`. It only requires `pool_data`, `pool_config`, `publisher`, `stake_account_positions`, `stake_account_custody`, `staking_program`, and `system_program`. [4](#0-3) 

Because `delegation_record` is not passed to `undelegate`, the program cannot check whether `nextSlashEventIndex < num_slash_events[publisher]` (i.e., whether there are unapplied slash events). A staker can therefore call `undelegate` after `create_slash_event` is confirmed on-chain but before `slash` is called on their account, closing their position with the full pre-slash amount.

The `advance_delegation_record` instruction — which processes both rewards and slashes for a staker — is similarly not enforced as a prerequisite to `undelegate`. [5](#0-4) 

---

### Impact Explanation

- Delegators who monitor the chain and react to `create_slash_event` can undelegate and withdraw their full stake, escaping the slash entirely.
- The DAO receives fewer slashed PYTH tokens than intended, weakening the economic security guarantee of OIS.
- Delegators who do not react (e.g., passive holders, those offline) bear the full slash on their remaining positions, creating an unequal distribution of the penalty — analogous to the H-7 "last to withdraw bears all the loss" pattern.
- The slashing rulebook explicitly states the slash is meant to be distributed uniformly pro-rata across all stakers in the pool; this mechanism breaks that guarantee. [6](#0-5) 

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- `create_slash_event` is a public on-chain transaction, immediately visible to all participants.
- Solana's block times (~400ms) give sophisticated stakers a realistic window to front-run the per-staker `slash` calls, especially since slashing hundreds of stakers requires many sequential transactions.
- Any delegator (unprivileged user) can call `undelegate` at any time — no special access required.
- The incentive is strong: a 5% slash on a large delegation is a significant financial loss to avoid. [7](#0-6) 

---

### Recommendation

The `undelegate` instruction should require the `delegation_record` account and enforce that `delegation_record.nextSlashEventIndex == pool_data.num_slash_events[publisher_index]` before allowing undelegation. Alternatively, `advance_delegation_record` (which applies pending slashes) should be called atomically as part of `undelegate`, similar to how many DeFi protocols require users to settle pending accounting before withdrawing.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Delegator Alice stakes 1,000,000 PYTH to Publisher P's pool.
2. A slashing event occurs. The `reward_program_authority` calls `create_slash_event(index=0, slash_ratio=500)` (5% slash). This is confirmed on-chain and visible to all.
3. Alice observes the `create_slash_event` transaction. She immediately calls `undelegate(position_index, 1_000_000)`. Because `undelegate` does not check `delegation_record.nextSlashEventIndex`, this succeeds.
4. Alice's position is closed. She enters cooldown with her full 1,000,000 PYTH.
5. When the `slash(index=0, ...)` instruction is later called for Alice's `stakeAccountPositions`, it either fails (position already closed) or has no effect.
6. Alice recovers 1,000,000 PYTH. Delegator Bob, who did not react, loses 50,000 PYTH. The DAO receives 50,000 PYTH instead of the intended 100,000 PYTH (assuming equal stakes).

```text
## Slashing

Slashing is an important aspect of the OIS protocol to ensure the integrity of the system.

The slashed amount for each pool is calculated as follows:

$$
\large{\bold{SL_p} = w \cdot S_p = w \cdot (S^{p}_{p} + S^{d}_{p})}
$$

Where:

- $SL_p$ is the slashed amount for the publisher $p$ pool.
- $w$ is the slashing rate.
- $S_p$ is the stake assigned to the publisher $p$ pool, made of self-staked amount $S^{p}_{p}$ and delegated stake $S^{d}_{p}$, or $S_{p} = S^{p}_{p} + S^{d}_{p}$.

Here $SL_p$ is uniformly allocated to both the self-staking publisher and delegators in the pool, pro-rata to their respective stake.
```
