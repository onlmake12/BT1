### Title
Permissionless Keeper Can Cherry-Pick Historical Price Updates to Trigger Spurious Deviation-Based Updates and Drain Subscription Balances — (`File: target_chains/ethereum/contracts/contracts/pulse/Scheduler.sol`)

---

### Summary

The `Scheduler.updatePriceFeeds` function is permissionless and accepts any valid Pyth price update within a broad staleness window (`[0, block.timestamp + FUTURE_TIMESTAMP_MAX_VALIDITY_PERIOD]`). For subscriptions with deviation-based trigger criteria, a malicious keeper can cherry-pick a historical price update from within the valid window that maximizes the apparent price deviation from the stored price — even when the current market price has not actually deviated past the threshold. This triggers spurious updates, drains the subscription's ETH balance (paid to the keeper), and stores a non-current price for readers.

---

### Finding Description

`Scheduler.updatePriceFeeds` is callable by anyone with no access restriction. [1](#0-0) 

Internally it calls `parsePriceFeedUpdatesWithConfig` with `minAllowedPublishTime = 0`: [2](#0-1) 

The only staleness enforcement is in `_validateShouldUpdatePrices`, which rejects updates older than `PAST_TIMESTAMP_MAX_VALIDITY_PERIOD` and older than the last stored update: [3](#0-2) 

Within the valid window `(priceLastUpdatedAt, block.timestamp - PAST_TIMESTAMP_MAX_VALIDITY_PERIOD]`, there can be many distinct Pyth price updates. The keeper supplies the `updateData` bytes and therefore controls which specific price update (which `publishTime` and price value) is submitted. The `checkUpdateDataIsMinimal = true` flag only enforces that exactly one update per price ID is included — it does not require the update to be the most recent one. [4](#0-3) 

For deviation-triggered subscriptions, `_validateShouldUpdatePrices` computes deviation against the stored price: [5](#0-4) 

A keeper can fetch from Hermes any price update published after `priceLastUpdatedAt` and within the staleness window. If the asset was volatile during that window (e.g., a flash spike and recovery), the keeper can select the spike price, which shows a large deviation from the stored price, satisfying the `deviationThresholdBps` check even though the current price has returned to normal.

The Pyth documentation itself acknowledges this class of risk under "Adversarial selection": [6](#0-5) 

The Scheduler, as Pyth's own production contract, does not apply the recommended mitigation (requiring the most recent price update).

---

### Impact Explanation

**Subscription balance drain**: Each successful `updatePriceFeeds` call pays the keeper from the subscription's ETH balance. By repeatedly triggering spurious deviation-based updates using cherry-picked historical prices, a malicious keeper can drain the subscription balance faster than intended. [7](#0-6) 

**Stale/misleading price stored for readers**: The subscription stores the cherry-picked historical price rather than the current market price. Readers using `getPricesUnsafe` or `getPricesNoOlderThan` receive a price that reflects a past spike/dip, not the current market state.

**Keeper economic extraction**: The keeper earns gas reimbursement plus a premium for each successful update, creating a direct financial incentive to exploit this.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- `updatePriceFeeds` is permissionless — any address can call it.
- Hermes provides a historical price API (`/v2/updates/price/{publish_time}`) that allows fetching any past price update, making cherry-picking trivial.
- Volatile assets (crypto, leveraged tokens) regularly exhibit intra-window spikes that exceed common deviation thresholds (e.g., 100 bps) before recovering.
- The keeper is economically incentivized: each successful call yields a payment from the subscription balance.
- No special knowledge, keys, or governance access is required.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Require the most recent update**: Pass `checkUniqueness = true` and enforce that the submitted price update's `publishTime` is within a tight window of `block.timestamp` (e.g., last 5–10 seconds), rather than accepting any update within `PAST_TIMESTAMP_MAX_VALIDITY_PERIOD`.

2. **Use `parsePriceFeedUpdatesUnique`** or pass `checkUniqueness = true` to `parsePriceFeedUpdatesWithConfig` with a narrow `[block.timestamp - N, block.timestamp + FUTURE_TIMESTAMP_MAX_VALIDITY_PERIOD]` window, ensuring the submitted update is the first (and thus most canonical) update after the minimum time.

3. **Alternatively**, validate on-chain that the submitted update's `publishTime` is within a few seconds of `block.timestamp`, rejecting historical cherry-picks.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. A subscription is created with `updateOnDeviation = true`, `deviationThresholdBps = 100` (1%), and `heartbeatSeconds = 3600`.
2. ETH/USD is stored at $2,000. The subscription's `priceLastUpdatedAt` = T₀.
3. At T₁ = T₀ + 30s, ETH spikes to $2,025 (+1.25%) then recovers to $2,005 by T₂ = T₀ + 60s.
4. A malicious keeper fetches the T₁ price update from Hermes (publishTime = T₁, price = $2,025).
5. The keeper calls `Scheduler.updatePriceFeeds(subscriptionId, updateData_T1)`.
6. `_validateShouldUpdatePrices` computes deviation: `|2025 - 2000| / 2000 = 125 bps ≥ 100 bps` → update is accepted.
7. The keeper receives gas reimbursement + premium from the subscription balance.
8. The subscription now stores $2,025 as the latest price, even though the current price is $2,005.
9. The keeper repeats: at T₃ = T₁ + 30s, they submit the T₂ update ($2,005), which shows `|2005 - 2025| / 2025 = 98 bps` — just under threshold. They wait for the next spike and repeat.
10. Over time, the subscription balance is drained at a rate far exceeding the intended heartbeat-based update frequency.

The root cause is in `Scheduler.sol` at the `parsePriceFeedUpdatesWithConfig` call with `minAllowedPublishTime = 0` and no requirement for the submitted update to be the most recent available. [8](#0-7)

## Adversarial selection

Pull updates give users of Pyth Network some ability to select which price to use in a transaction.
This ability is highly circumscribed by various constraints: on-chain prices must move forward in time and cannot be from too far in the past.
However, users can still choose any price update that satisfies these constraints.
This ability is functionally equivalent to latency: it allows users to see the price in the future before using a price from the past.
