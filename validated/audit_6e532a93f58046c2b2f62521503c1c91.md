### Title
Unprivileged Caller Can Front-Run `set_publisher_stake_account` to Hijack Publisher Stake Association — (`governance/pyth_staking_sdk/src/idl/integrity-pool.json`)

---

### Summary

The `set_publisher_stake_account` instruction in the Oracle Integrity Staking (OIS) integrity-pool program accepts any arbitrary signer. There is no on-chain constraint requiring the signer to be the publisher themselves or a governance authority. An unprivileged attacker can call this instruction before a legitimate publisher, associating an attacker-controlled stake account with any registered publisher. This corrupts the publisher's self-stake accounting, redirects publisher rewards to the attacker, and can permanently block the publisher from registering their legitimate stake account.

---

### Finding Description

The `set_publisher_stake_account` instruction is defined in the integrity-pool program IDL with the following account structure:

- `signer` — only constrained as `signer: true`; no `relations`, `has_one`, or authority constraint
- `publisher` — annotated `CHECK: The publisher will be checked against data in the pool_data`; **not a signer**
- `pool_data` / `pool_config` — standard PDA accounts
- `new_stake_account_positions_option` — optional, no ownership constraint
- `current_stake_account_positions_option` — optional, no ownership constraint

The `signer` account has no Anchor `relations` or `has_one` constraint linking it to the `publisher` account or to any authority field in `pool_config`. The `publisher` account is explicitly not required to sign. This means any wallet can be the `signer` and pass any registered publisher pubkey as `publisher`.

The IDL comment on `publisher` says only that the publisher key is verified to exist in `pool_data` (i.e., it is a registered publisher). It says nothing about verifying that the signer has authority over that publisher. The absence of an Anchor-level constraint is the root cause: in Anchor, authority checks that are enforced at the constraint layer appear in the IDL as `relations` or `has_one` fields. No such field exists here.

The analog to the external report is direct:
- **External report**: Bob front-runs the operator's first `stake()` call; Bob's funds are misinterpreted as the operator's bond deposit, and the operator can bypass their bond.
- **Pyth analog**: Attacker front-runs the publisher's first `set_publisher_stake_account` call; the attacker's stake account is registered as the publisher's self-stake account, corrupting reward accounting and cap calculations. [1](#0-0) 

The xc_admin CLI uses the governance vault PDA as the signer, confirming the intended caller is privileged — but the on-chain program does not enforce this. [2](#0-1) 

The TypeScript SDK's `setPublisherStakeAccount` method passes `this.wallet.publicKey` as the implicit signer with no additional authority check, confirming the instruction is callable by any wallet. [3](#0-2) 

---

### Impact Explanation

1. **Publisher reward theft**: Publisher rewards in OIS are distributed to the associated stake account's custody. If an attacker's stake account is registered, publisher epoch rewards flow to the attacker's custody instead of the legitimate publisher's.

2. **Publisher cap manipulation**: A publisher's delegation cap is proportional to their self-stake. An attacker can register a zero-balance stake account, reducing the publisher's cap to zero and preventing any delegator from staking to that publisher — effectively griefing the publisher out of the OIS system.

3. **Permanent lock-out**: If the program body checks that `current_stake_account_positions_option` matches the currently stored account before allowing a reassignment (the standard pattern), the publisher cannot override the attacker's registration because the publisher does not control the attacker's stake account. The publisher's legitimate stake account can never be registered.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- The instruction is permissionless and callable by any Solana wallet with no special privileges.
- The publisher's pubkey is visible on-chain in `pool_data`, so an attacker can enumerate all registered publishers.
- A publisher who has not yet called `set_publisher_stake_account` is permanently vulnerable until they do so.
- The attack requires only a single transaction and minimal SOL for fees.
- Front-running is straightforward on Solana by monitoring the mempool or simply racing the publisher's setup transaction.

---

### Recommendation

Add an Anchor `has_one` or `constraint` requiring the signer to equal the publisher pubkey, or require the signer to be the `reward_program_authority` stored in `pool_config`. For example, in the Rust context struct:

```rust
#[account(
    constraint = signer.key() == publisher.key() || signer.key() == pool_config.reward_program_authority
)]
pub signer: Signer<'info>,
```

Alternatively, if this is intended to be governance-only, add a `has_one = reward_program_authority` constraint on `signer` against `pool_config`. [4](#0-3) 

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Observe that publisher `P` is registered in `pool_data` but has not yet called `set_publisher_stake_account` (their stake account field is `None`).
2. Attacker creates their own stake account `A` via the trustless `create_stake_account` instruction (explicitly permissionless per its own IDL docs).
3. Attacker calls `set_publisher_stake_account` with:
   - `signer` = attacker's wallet (any valid signer)
   - `publisher` = `P` (registered publisher, passes the pool_data check)
   - `new_stake_account_positions_option` = `A` (attacker's stake account)
   - `current_stake_account_positions_option` = `None`
4. `pool_data` now records `A` as publisher `P`'s stake account.
5. All subsequent `advance_delegation_record` calls for publisher `P` credit rewards to `A`'s custody.
6. Publisher `P` attempts to call `set_publisher_stake_account` with their legitimate account `L`, but the program requires `current_stake_account_positions_option = A`. Since `P` does not own `A`, they cannot authorize the change, and the association is permanently locked to the attacker's account. [1](#0-0) [5](#0-4)
