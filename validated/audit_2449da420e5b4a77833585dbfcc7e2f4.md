### Title
Unprivileged Front-Running of `post_vaa` via Attacker-Controlled `signature_set` Permanently Blocks Legitimate Relayer and Locks Their SOL — (`target_chains/solana/programs/core-bridge/src/legacy/processor/post_vaa.rs`)

---

### Summary

The legacy `post_vaa` instruction derives the `posted_vaa` PDA solely from `signature_set.message_hash` and uses `init` (not `init_if_needed`). Because the `signature_set` account is an arbitrary keypair (not a PDA tied to the payer), any unprivileged actor can create their own `SignatureSet` for the same message hash, front-run the legitimate relayer's `post_vaa` call, and permanently occupy the PDA slot. The legitimate relayer's transaction then fails with `AccountAlreadyInitialized`, their pre-paid `signature_set` SOL is permanently locked, and the posted VAA's `signature_set` field points to the attacker's account.

---

### Finding Description

**Step 1 — `signature_set` is a free keypair, not a payer-bound PDA.**

In `verify_signatures.rs`, the `signature_set` account is declared with `init_if_needed` and no seeds — it is a plain system-program keypair account: [1](#0-0) 

The attacker generates `Keypair::new()`, signs the `verify_signatures` transactions with it (the keypair must sign to initialize the account), and populates it with the same guardian signatures extracted from the publicly broadcast VAA.

**Step 2 — `post_vaa` PDA is seeded only by `message_hash`, not by payer or signature_set pubkey.** [2](#0-1) 

Two callers with different `signature_set` keypairs but the same VAA body will compute the identical PDA address. The first `init` wins; the second fails with `AccountAlreadyInitialized`.

**Step 3 — `signature_set` is not a signer in `post_vaa`.** [3](#0-2) 

No ownership or signer check is enforced on the `signature_set` account passed to `post_vaa`. Any account owned by the Core Bridge program with a matching `message_hash` and quorum passes.

**Step 4 — The only guard is a hardcoded 16-entry blocklist.** [4](#0-3) 

This is a reactive, finite denylist. Any freshly generated keypair bypasses it entirely.

**Step 5 — The attacker's `signature_set` pubkey is written into `PostedVaaV1Info`.** [5](#0-4) 

**Step 6 — `close_signature_set` enforces `has_one = signature_set`.** [6](#0-5) 

Because the posted VAA now stores the attacker's `signature_set` pubkey, the legitimate relayer's `signature_set` account can never be closed through this path. Their rent SOL is permanently locked.

---

### Impact Explanation

| Affected party | Effect |
|---|---|
| Legitimate relayer | `post_vaa` fails permanently (`AccountAlreadyInitialized`); cannot retry |
| Legitimate relayer | SOL paid to create their `signature_set` is permanently locked (no `close_signature_set` path available) |
| Posted VAA | `PostedVaaV1Info.signature_set` points to attacker-controlled account |
| Attacker | Can immediately close their own `signature_set` and reclaim their SOL, making the attack nearly free |

The VAA body content is correct (the message hash check at lines 114–128 ensures this), so Pyth price feed consumption is not directly broken. However, the relayer griefing and SOL loss are concrete and permanent.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Guardian signatures are embedded in the publicly broadcast VAA bytes. Any observer can extract them, create a fresh keypair, replay the `verify_signatures` sequence with that keypair, and submit `post_vaa` before the legitimate relayer. The `verify_signatures` step requires multiple transactions (one per 7-signature batch), but on Solana these can be pre-staged. The attack cost is only the transaction fees and the rent for the attacker's `signature_set` (reclaimed immediately after). The existence of the 16-entry `INVALID_SIGNATURE_SET_KEYS` blocklist is direct evidence that this class of attack has already been observed in production.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Bind the `posted_vaa` PDA to the payer** — include `payer.key()` in the PDA seeds, or require the `signature_set` to be a PDA derived from `(payer, message_hash)`.
2. **Alternatively, use `init_if_needed` with an idempotency check** — if the account already exists and its `message_hash` matches, return success instead of erroring. The original implementation did this (see the comment at lines 53–55); restoring that behavior eliminates the permanent-block impact.
3. **Migrate to the new `init_encoded_vaa` / `process_encoded_vaa` path** — the new Anchor instructions do not rely on the legacy `SignatureSet` keypair pattern and are not subject to this race.

---

### Proof of Concept

```
1. Observe a VAA being relayed (e.g., a Pyth governance VAA).
2. attacker_keypair = Keypair::new()  // attacker's signature_set
3. Call verify_signatures_txs(vaa_bytes, ..., attacker_keypair.pubkey())
   → signs each tx with [payer, attacker_keypair]
   → populates attacker's SignatureSet with quorum of guardian sigs
4. Call post_vaa(wormhole, attacker_payer, attacker_keypair.pubkey(), post_vaa_data)
   → posted_vaa PDA = PDA["PostedVAA", message_hash] is created
   → PostedVaaV1Info.signature_set = attacker_keypair.pubkey()
5. Legitimate relayer's post_vaa arrives → AccountAlreadyInitialized
6. Legitimate relayer's signature_set account is permanently stuck
   (close_signature_set fails: has_one = signature_set mismatch)
7. Attacker calls close_signature_set with their own signature_set → reclaims SOL
```
